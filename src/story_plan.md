# KPSI Story Plan — Ἡ Κοινὴ καθ᾿ αὑτὴν φωτιζομένη

## Narrative premise

Corinth, 51 AD. **Χρύσιππος** (Chrysippus) is a Greek freedman in his mid-thirties
— formerly a slave in the household of a Roman merchant, now freed and running a
modest stall in the Corinthian agora selling rope, lamp oil, and odds and ends
imported from the East. He is not wealthy. He is not clever in the way he thinks he
is. He is earnest, easily distracted, occasionally pompous, and fundamentally
decent. He is the kind of man who has strong opinions about which harbour is best
and who will share those opinions at length with anyone who will listen.

His household is small. His world is the agora, the harbour at Lechaion, the road
east to Cenchreae, the tavern of one **Δάμων** (Damon), and — increasingly — the
gathering of a strange new group in the house of **Κρίσπος** (Crispus), a synagogue
official he barely knows.

The comedy is low-register: misunderstandings, bad deals, social embarrassments,
domestic disputes about a donkey, a persistent creditor named **Θεόφραστος**
(Theophrastus). The theology arrives slowly, absorbed before it is understood.

## Narrative arc (50 chapters total)

### Act I — Corinth (Chapters 1–13): *Who is Chrysippus?*

**Tone:** comic, grounded, domestic. Grammar: εἰμί → present active → dative/genitive → 3rd decl. consonant stems → 3rd decl. neuter/i-stems → deponents → relative pronoun → aspect framing → imperfect.

- Ch 1: The Roman world. Chrysippus introduced at the end as a man in a large city.
- Ch 2: The agora. Chrysippus's stall; his goods; his neighbours. First transitive verbs.
- Ch 3: The household. Melitta, Xanthias, Lykos. Direct speech begins.
- Ch 4: The harbour of Lechaion. Ships arrive; Chrysippus negotiates badly.
- Ch 5: A creditor arrives. Theophrastus wants money. Chrysippus has none.
- Ch 6: **Consolidation — no new grammar.** A festival day in the agora; all Ch 1–5 vocabulary recycled at high density. First *Ἐν ταῖς γραφαῖς* section with multiple NT texts read together.
- Ch 7: The Syrian merchant arrives with cheaper oil. Chrysippus borrows fifty drachmas from Damon; Damon's father disapproves. **3rd decl. consonant stems: ἀνήρ, νύξ, χάρις, πατήρ, σάρξ, χείρ; πᾶς.**
- Ch 8: Melitta meets Priscilla at the well; sells the cloth; Chrysippus passes Aquila's workshop. The theological vocabulary of the story begins to appear. **3rd decl. neuters and i-stems: σῶμα, πνεῦμα, πρᾶγμα, ὄνομα, δύναμις, πίστις; gen. of price.**
- Ch 9: Melitta sends Chrysippus for a barley loaf; he returns with an expensive wheat loaf via a logical process that convinces no one. **Present middle/passive deponents: πορεύομαι, ἔρχομαι, γίνομαι, ἀποκρίνομαι, ἐρωτάω.**
- Ch 10: Chrysippus searches Corinth for Xanthias; finds him at Cenchreae watching a ship from Lydia. The lit house on the way home. **Relative pronoun ὅς/ἥ/ὅ all cases; embedded relative clauses.**
- Ch 11: Damon's tavern. Damon narrates his life history entirely in imperfect; Chrysippus tries to interject quick facts. **Aspect framing: τὸ παρατατικόν introduced as label; minimal pair demonstrated; no Greek prose definition yet.**
- Ch 12: Chrysippus searches the synagogue district. **Imperfect indicative: ἐζήτει, ἐβλέπεν, ᾔδει, ἦγεν; narrative reconstruction.**
- Ch 13: Chrysippus outside the community door. He hears but does not enter. Melitta says she wants to go. **Relative pronoun recycling; Act I closes.**

### Act II — The community (Chapters 11–20): *Who are these people?*

**Tone:** shifts from purely comic to comic-serious. The Pauline community enters.
Grammar: aorist (the critical unlock) → future → participles → ἵνα + subjunctive → imperatives.

- Ch 11: Chrysippus visits the harbour at Cenchreae. He meets Aquila. Aorist introduced.
- Ch 12: Aquila and Priscilla's workshop. Chrysippus is offered work. He declines.
- Ch 13: Theophrastus confronts Chrysippus publicly in the agora. Aorist middle/passive.
- Ch 14: Melitta visits Priscilla. She comes home changed. Chrysippus is suspicious.
- Ch 15: A ship from Ephesus arrives with bad news: a debt Chrysippus forgot he had. Future.
- Ch 16: Chrysippus attends the synagogue by accident. Crispus reads. Present participles.
- Ch 17: Chrysippus leaves quickly. But he remembers what was read. Aorist participles.
- Ch 18: Damon gets sick. Chrysippus visits. He doesn't know what to say. Substantival participle.
- Ch 19: Melitta tells Chrysippus to go back to the meeting. ἵνα + subjunctive.
- Ch 20: Paul arrives in Corinth. Chrysippus sees him in the agora. Imperatives; articular infinitive.

### Act III — Conviction (Chapters 21–28): *What is true?*

**Tone:** serious with comic relief. Chrysippus is being changed without fully consenting to it.
Grammar: perfect → genitive absolute → conditionals → ὅτι discourse → periphrastic → Hebraistic parataxis.

- Ch 21: Chrysippus attends a full gathering. He hears Paul speak. Perfect indicative.
- Ch 22: A crisis: Sosthenes is beaten before the governor (Acts 18:17). Chrysippus watches. Perfect M/P; perfect participle.
- Ch 23: Crispus's household is baptised (Acts 18:8). Chrysippus is outside the door. Genitive absolute.
- Ch 24: Theophrastus's daughter is ill. Chrysippus doesn't know what to do. Conditionals.
- Ch 25: Paul teaches about love (1 Cor 13 in summary). Chrysippus reports to Melitta. ὅτι discourse.
- Ch 26: Melitta decides. The household discussion. Periphrastic constructions.
- Ch 27: Damon gets better. No one can explain why. Pluperfect.
- Ch 28: Stephanas's household. Parataxis; the language of testimony.

### Act IV — Arrival (Chapters 29–30): *What now?*

- Ch 29: Chrysippus prays for the first time (optative wishes: εἴη, μὴ γένοιτο).
- Ch 30: Chloe's people arrive with letters. The grammar is now complete. Paul leaves Corinth (Acts 18:18). Chrysippus watches the ship depart.

### Act V — Real texts (Chapters 31–35)

- Ch 31–32: 1 John 1–5 (unadapted).
- Ch 33–34: Mark — selected narratives.
- Ch 35: 1 Corinthians 13 (unadapted). The reader has arrived.

## Chapter-by-chapter plan: Acts I–II in detail

### Chapter 1 — Ἡ οἰκουμένη (The inhabited world)
**Grammar:** εἰμί all forms; nom case; article; 1st/2nd decl. nom; prepositional phrases with dat/gen (passive); μέγας, μικρός, πολύς; particles.
**Scene:** Geography → Roman world → Corinth → Chrysippus (one paragraph, three sentences).
**Tone:** encyclopaedic, gradually human.
**Vocab introduced:** ὁ/ἡ/τό, εἰμί, ἡ Ἑλλάς, ἡ πόλις, ὁ ἄνθρωπος, ἡ θάλασσα, ἡ ὁδός, τὸ πλοῖον, ἡ ἐπαρχία, ἡ ἀρχή, ἐγγύς, μέγας, μικρός, πολύς, ναί, οὐχί, ποῦ, τί, ἆρα, καί, δέ, μέν, ἀλλά, γάρ.

### Chapter 2 — Ἡ ἀγορά (The market)
**Grammar:** Accusative (1st/2nd decl.); present indicative active (3rd sg/pl first, then all persons by end); basic transitive verbs (ἔχω, βλέπω, ἀγοράζω, πωλέω, φέρω); τίς/τί interrogative.
**Scene:** The Corinthian agora on a weekday morning. Chrysippus opens his stall. He has rope, lamp-oil, a jar of something he isn't sure about. His neighbour **Νίκων** (Nikon), a sardine-seller, greets him with contempt. A woman examines one of his ropes and puts it down. Chrysippus has opinions about this.
**Comedy:** Chrysippus tries to sell the unknown jar. He doesn't know what is in it. Nikon says it is fish paste. Chrysippus insists it is not. A customer buys it for very little.
**New vocab (15–17):** ἡ ἀγορά, ἔχω, βλέπω, φέρω, ὁ ἄρτος, τὸ σκεῦος, ὁ λόγος, ἡ τιμή, μέγας (acc), ὁ κύριος, ἡ γυνή, λαμβάνω, τίς/τί (interrog.), οὐδείς, πολύς (acc).
**Recycled from Ch1:** πόλις, ἄνθρωπος, εἰμί, μέγας, μικρός, ὁδός, Κόρινθος.
**Narrative hook:** Chrysippus hears that a man named Aquila has arrived from Rome and is looking for workspace in the agora.

---

### Chapter 3 — Ἡ οἰκία (The household)
**Grammar:** Dative (1st/2nd decl.); indirect object; λέγω/λαλέω + dative; 1st/2nd person present (ἔχεις, ἔχομεν, λέγω, λέγεις); vocative.
**Scene:** Chrysippus comes home. The house: one room for goods, a courtyard, a cooking fire. Melitta is cooking. Xanthias is supposedly sweeping (he is not sweeping). Lykos the donkey is in the courtyard for no clear reason.
**Comedy:** Chrysippus tells Melitta he almost sold a mystery jar for a good price. She says: you sold it for almost nothing. He says: yes, but almost. Lykos eats something he shouldn't. Xanthias is blamed.
**New vocab (15–17):** ἡ οἰκία, ὁ οἶκος, λέγω, λαλέω, ἀκούω, ἡ θύρα, ἡ αὐλή, τὸ ὕδωρ, ὁ δοῦλος, ὁ δεσπότης, ἡ κεφαλή, ὁ ὄνος, καλέω, εἰσέρχομαι (stem only, not conjugated fully yet), ἴδε/ἰδού.
**Recycled from Ch1–2:** ἄνθρωπος, γυνή, κύριος, λαμβάνω, εἰμί, λόγος.
**Narrative hook:** Melitta has heard from a neighbour that a Jewish couple — tentmakers — have taken a workshop near the agora. She is curious. Chrysippus is not curious.

---

### Chapter 4 — Τὸ λιμάνιον (The harbour)
**Grammar:** Genitive (1st/2nd decl.): possession, source, separation; ἐκ/ἀπό + gen expanded; comparative adjective with ἤ; πρός + acc (toward/to).
**Scene:** Chrysippus goes to Lechaion, Corinth's western harbour, to meet a ship from Ephesus that (he believes) carries the rope he ordered. It does not. It carries a large quantity of cloth. He is shown a document. He cannot read all of it. He negotiates.
**Comedy:** The ship's agent is Greek but speaks with an Egyptian accent Chrysippus finds impenetrable. Xanthias translates, badly. The donkey Lykos, brought to carry rope, now has to carry cloth instead. He expresses displeasure.
**New vocab (15–17):** τὸ πλοῖον (acc/gen now active), ὁ λιμήν, ἐκ, ὁ ἔμπορος, τὸ ἱμάτιον, ὁ δοῦλος (gen/dat now), ἡ ἐπιστολή, γράφω, ἀναγινώσκω, ὁ ἄγγελος (= messenger here, not yet "angel"), μικρός (comp: μικρότερος), πλείων, νῦν, τότε, ἐκεῖ.
**Recycled:** πλοῖον, θάλασσα, ὁδός, ἔχω, φέρω, γυνή, οἰκία.
**Narrative hook:** At the harbour Chrysippus sees Aquila — the man from Rome — examining a cargo. They do not speak, but Chrysippus notices he is a careful, unhurried man.

---

### Chapter 5 — Ὁ χρεώστης (The debtor)
**Grammar:** Personal pronouns full (ἐγώ, σύ, ἡμεῖς, ὑμεῖς) in all cases; demonstratives οὗτος/αὕτη/τοῦτο; ἐκεῖνος; αὐτός as emphatic "self."
**Scene:** Theophrastus arrives at Chrysippus's stall. He is a respectable man with a wax tablet on which things are written. He is owed money. Chrysippus owes him money. This is not a surprise to either of them but it is still unpleasant.
**Comedy:** Theophrastus enumerates the debt formally. Chrysippus has counter-arguments, none of them good. Melitta appears and agrees with Theophrastus about everything. Chrysippus feels betrayed. Xanthias, asked his opinion, offers none, but his face says enough. Theophrastus leaves. Chrysippus reassures himself that he has a plan.
**New vocab (15–17):** ἐγώ/σύ (all cases), ἡμεῖς/ὑμεῖς, οὗτος/αὕτη/τοῦτο, ἐκεῖνος, αὐτός (emphatic), ὁ χρεώστης [gloss: debtor — not NT-500], τὸ ἀργύριον, ὀφείλω, σήμερον, αὔριον, οὐκέτι, ὅλος, ἀλήθεια.
**Recycled:** λέγω, ἀκούω, γυνή, δοῦλος, κύριος, ἔχω, θύρα, οἰκία.
**Narrative hook:** Theophrastus, leaving, pauses and says that he heard the Jewish tentmaker gave a short address at the synagogue. He says this not warmly, but not coldly either. It is information.

---

### Chapter 6 — Ἡ ἑορτή (The Festival)
**Grammar:** CONSOLIDATION — no new grammar. Zero new lemmas introduced.
**Scene:** A festival day in the Corinthian agora. Business is suspended; the stalls are shuttered. Chrysippus walks the market with time on his hands for once. He talks with Nikon; they argue about whose fish smell worse in warm weather. Melitta has already finished the household accounts. Lykos has eaten a wreath that was not his.
**Comedy:** Chrysippus attempts to use his free time productively and fails at several things in sequence. Nikon offers to share his lunch. Chrysippus accepts while explaining at length why he does not need charity.
**New vocab:** 0. This chapter introduces no new lemmas. Every word from Ch 1–5 that has not appeared since its introduction chapter must appear here — the chapter functions as an explicit atrophy check.
**Recycled from Ch 1–5:** All major nouns (ἄνθρωπος, γυνή, κύριος, δοῦλος, οἰκία, ἀγορά, θύρα, τιμή, λόγος, ἀργύριον), all present active forms, pronoun and demonstrative sets, εἰμί, particle set.
**Narrative hook:** A neighbour mentions that a Jewish tentmaker named Ἀκύλας has arrived from Rome with his wife. They have taken a workshop near the tanneries. Chrysippus says this is not interesting.
***Ἐν ταῖς γραφαῖς*:** 1 John 1:5 and 4:8 read together for the first time: *ὁ θεὸς φῶς ἐστίν... ὁ θεὸς ἀγάπη ἐστίν.* Then John 1:1: *ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος.* All content words now met in the main text. The αἱ λέξεις section asks: τί ἐστιν ὁ θεός; — and leaves the reader with the two answers side by side.

---

### Chapter 7 — Ὁ ξένος (The Stranger)
**Grammar:** 3rd declension consonant stems: ἀνήρ/ἀνδρός, νύξ/νυκτός, χάρις/χάριτος, πατήρ/πατρός, σάρξ/σαρκός, χείρ/χειρός; πᾶς/πάντος.
**Scene:** A Syrian merchant with a cart of lamp oil sets up directly next to Chrysippus's stall. He is larger, louder, and has better oil at a better price. Chrysippus develops a series of arguments about why this is not a problem. None of them work. That evening Chrysippus visits Damon and borrows fifty drachmas. The terms are discussed in the presence of Damon's father (πατήρ — disapproving). The deal involves χάρις (the word is used formally), is struck at night (νύξ), and Chrysippus shakes the man's χείρ. Melitta takes charge of the money before Chrysippus has finished celebrating.
**Comedy:** The Syrian merchant never speaks directly to Chrysippus; he only smiles at everything Chrysippus says, which is worse. Damon's father has objections. Chrysippus assures everyone he has a plan. He does not have a plan.
**New vocab (15–17):** ἀνήρ/ἀνδρός, νύξ/νυκτός, χάρις/χάριτος, πατήρ/πατρός, σάρξ/σαρκός, χείρ/χειρός, πᾶς/πάντος, ὁ χρόνος, ἕκαστος, ἄλλος, ὅμοιος, μόνος, κρείσσων [gloss: μεγαλότερος τῇ τιμῇ].
**Recycled from Ch 1–6:** ἄνθρωπος, λέγω, ἔχω, τιμή, ἀργύριον, οἰκία, γυνή, ὀφείλω, νῦν, σήμερον.
**Narrative hook:** Nikon tells Chrysippus that Aquila is still looking for a reliable rope supplier. Chrysippus is not interested in a man who worships a foreign god. Nikon says Aquila's wife is Greek.
***Ἐν ταῖς γραφαῖς*:** Acts 18:1–2 (*μετὰ ταῦτα... εὗρεν τινα Ἰουδαῖον ὀνόματι Ἀκύλαν, Ποντικὸν τῷ γένει*). All 3rd decl. forms pointed in the margin with their nominatives. The student meets the historical Aquila for the first time in the actual source text.

### Chapter 8 — Τὸ σῶμα καὶ ἡ ψυχή (Body and Soul)
**Grammar:** 3rd declension neuter stems and i-stems: σῶμα/σώματος, πνεῦμα/πνεύματος, πρᾶγμα/πράγματος, ὄνομα/ὀνόματος, δύναμις/δυνάμεως, πίστις/πίστεως. Genitive of price recycled (from Ch 4) now with 3rd decl. nouns. Meta-vocab introduced in αἱ λέξεις: τὸ γένος (gender), ἡ πτῶσις (case).
**Scene:** Melitta has already sold the cloth by the time Chrysippus thinks to ask about it. She says only that she got a fair price. Chrysippus, attempting to sell his oil, learns that Aquila is known in the market as an honest man who needs rope for his workshop. He walks past Aquila's workshop on the way home — he can hear voices inside — but does not stop. Xanthias, sent to the well, meets Priscilla; she speaks to him kindly and uses his name, though they have never met.
**Comedy:** Chrysippus rehearses a speech about rope quality and expertise while walking. He delivers none of it. He tells Melitta he was not in that part of the city.
**New vocab (15–17):** σῶμα, πνεῦμα, πρᾶγμα, ὄνομα, δύναμις, πίστις, ἔθνος, κόσμος, αἷμα, ἐλπίς, ῥῆμα, γνῶσις, ἔργον.
**Recycled from Ch 1–7:** All consonant-stem nouns from Ch 7; γυνή, δοῦλος, ὄνομα (now in narrative use); πωλέω, ἔχω, βλέπω.
**Narrative hook:** That evening Melitta tells Chrysippus that she and Priscilla spoke briefly. She gives no details beyond this. Chrysippus asks one question and receives an answer that answers nothing.
***Ἐν ταῖς γραφαῖς*:** Acts 18:3–4. The αἱ λέξεις section introduces τὸ γένος (ἀρσενικόν, θηλυκόν, οὐδέτερον) and ἡ πτῶσις using σῶμα as the paradigm noun, with a small table of nominative and genitive forms for all three genders.

### Chapter 9 — Τὸ ἱμάτιον (The Cloak)
**Grammar:** Present middle/passive deponents: πορεύομαι, ἔρχομαι, γίνομαι, ἀποκρίνομαι, ἐρωτάω. The αἱ λέξεις section explains the category using minimal terms: the form is middle but the meaning is active — demonstrated by contrasting paired sentences, not by abstract rule.
**Scene:** Melitta sends Chrysippus to the baker on the left side of the street for a small barley loaf. Chrysippus meets Euboulides the lamp-seller on the way and loses count of lefts. He arrives at the right baker through a process of elimination that makes sense to no one but him. He returns with a large, expensive wheat loaf. Melitta is not surprised. Xanthias has witnessed the entire journey from a discreet distance and says nothing; his expression says enough.
**Comedy:** Euboulides's directions are perfectly clear. Chrysippus misapplies them systematically, reverses his logic at the last moment, and presents the wheat loaf as evidence that his method was correct.
**New vocab (15–17):** πορεύομαι, ἔρχομαι, γίνομαι, ἀποκρίνομαι, ἐρωτάω, ὁ ἄρτος, εὐθύς, μακράν, πάλιν, ἀριστερά, δεξιά, ὅπου, ἐκεῖθεν, μικρός (now in opposition to μέγας in a single sentence).
**Recycled from Ch 1–8:** πόλις, ἀγορά, γυνή, δοῦλος, λέγω, ἀκούω, βλέπω, εἰμί, ὁδός, σμικρός/μικρός, μέγας, all nouns from Ch 7–8.
**Narrative hook:** Xanthias mentions having seen Aquila and a kind-looking woman at the well. Melitta says: I know. She has known for some days.
***Ἐν ταῖς γραφαῖς*:** Luke 2:8–11 (*ποιμένες ἦσαν ἐν τῇ χώρᾳ... καὶ εἶπεν αὐτοῖς ὁ ἄγγελος· μὴ φοβεῖσθε*). πορεύεσθε appears in v.15 — pointed as a deponent. εἶπεν is glossed in the margin: *εἶπεν — ἀόριστος τοῦ λέγω — κεφάλαιον ΙΕ* (to be learned in Chapter 15).

### Chapter 10 — Ξανθίας (Xanthias)
**Grammar:** Relative pronoun ὅς/ἥ/ὅ, all cases in all genders. Agreement with antecedent shown before the full embedded-clause pattern is used. The αἱ λέξεις section names it: ἡ ἀναφορικὴ ἀντωνυμία.
**Scene:** Chrysippus returns home to find Xanthias gone. He searches Corinth — the agora (ἡ ἀγορά, ἐν ᾗ ὁ Νίκων...), the harbour road (ἡ ὁδός, ἣν γιγνώσκει...). He finds Xanthias at the Cenchreae docks watching a ship from Lydia — his homeland. On the way back through the city they pass a house from which light comes and from which voices can be heard. Xanthias says: *οἶκός ἐστιν ἐν ᾧ φῶς ἐστιν ἑκάστης ἑσπέρας.* Chrysippus says he is not curious. Melitta, when told, says she wants to go.
**Comedy:** Chrysippus's search is systematic and wrong. He finds Xanthias immediately but pauses before speaking because he has prepared a speech about responsibility. The speech dissolves when he actually looks at Xanthias's face watching the ship.
**New vocab (15–17):** The relative pronoun paradigm (as meta-term: ἡ ἀναφορικὴ ἀντωνυμία); φῶς/φωτός (now formally introduced as content vocabulary — met in glosses since Ch 1); λύχνος, ἑσπέρα, ἕκαστος, ἐκεῖθεν, ἡ πατρίς [gloss: ἡ γῆ ἐξ ἧς ὁ ἄνθρωπός ἐστιν], ἐλεύθερος, φαίνω, ἀεί.
**Recycled from Ch 1–9:** All deponents from Ch 9 (ἔρχεται, πορεύεται, γίνεται); ζητέω, βλέπω, εὑρίσκω, ἀκούω, λέγω, πόλις, ἀγορά, θάλασσα, ὁδός, πλοῖον.
**Narrative hook:** The lit house belongs to Κρίσπος — the synagogue ruler. The community meets there every evening. Chrysippus says he has no interest in Jewish customs. Melitta says nothing more.
***Ἐν ταῖς γραφαῖς*:** Acts 18:5–6 and **Ps 110:1 LXX** (*εἶπεν ὁ κύριος τῷ κυρίῳ μου· κάθου ἐκ δεξιῶν μου, ἕως ἂν θῶ τοὺς ἐχθρούς σου ὑποπόδιον τῶν ποδῶν σου*). Relative-clause structure visible throughout. **First LXX seed.** Listed NT cross-references: Mark 12:36; Acts 2:34–35; Heb 1:13; Rom 8:34.

## Vocabulary policy

- **Primary constraint:** NT Top-500 lemmas by frequency.
- **Extended vocab (LXX/Didache/Apostolic Fathers):** Permitted *only* when NT vocab alone cannot carry the narrative. Must be:
  1. Glossed with `.gloss` tooltip in HTML.
  2. Noted in the αἱ λέξεις section with provenance (e.g. "[LXX]").
  3. Never introduced at the expense of NT-500 recycling targets.
- **Recycling rule:** Every introduced word appears 5–8× in its introduction chapter, then every 2–3 chapters thereafter. Atrophy threshold: 4+ chapters without appearance = flagged.

## Quality standard

- Morphologically correct Koine throughout (BDF + Wallace).
- Fully polytonic, correctly accented.
- Authentic Koine syntax: ἵνα not infinitive for purpose; ὅτι recitative; Hebraistic parataxis where narratively natural.
- No Atticism: no classical optative in place of subjunctive for conditions; no classical negation patterns.
