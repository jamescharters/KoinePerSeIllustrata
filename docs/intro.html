Introduction

Ἡ Κοινὴ καθ᾿ αὑτὴν φωτιζομένη

What it is, what it aims to do, and how to use it

KPSI is a graded reader for Koine Greek designed around a single goal: to bring the learner to fluent, unaided reading of the Greek New Testament through comprehensible input rather than explicit grammar instruction.

The method follows that of Hans Ørberg's Lingua Latina Per Se Illustrata and Seumas MacDonald's Lingua Graeca Per Se Illustrata: new vocabulary and grammar are introduced in context, made unambiguous by the situation, and recycled throughout subsequent chapters until fully automatic. No word lists to memorise. No paradigm tables to drill. Only reading.

The narrative. The story is set in Corinth in the 50s AD — a cosmopolitan Roman colony and the setting of Paul's first letter. The central character is a fictional freedman merchant, his household, and those they encounter in a city where Greek, Roman, and Jewish worlds converge. As the story develops, the reader enters the world of the New Testament before ever opening it.

Vocabulary. All chapters are constrained strictly to the top 500 lemmas by frequency in the Greek New Testament, covering approximately 90% of all word occurrences. Words outside this tier are glossed in the margin when they appear. By the end of the narrative section (Chapter 30), the reader will have encountered every word in this tier multiple times, in varied contexts, across the full range of NT grammar.

Grammar. Every major NT construction is introduced inductively: aorist indicative, participles (present and aorist), ἵνα + subjunctive, genitive absolute, periphrastic constructions, conditional sentences, and the optative mood. All four cases — nominative, accusative, genitive, and dative — are introduced across the first ten chapters and used extensively throughout. The verbal system is covered in full: present, imperfect, future, aorist, and perfect indicative in active, middle, and passive voices, with every major conjugation class represented. The grammar is complete by Chapter 30. Chapters 31–35 transition to real, unadapted NT texts beginning with 1 John.

Can I use this to teach myself Greek? That is precisely the aim. It is not yet complete, but what exists is designed to be used from the very first page without any prior knowledge of Greek.
